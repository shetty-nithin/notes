1. Why GNN are required?
    Most real world data isn't a table of independent rows. Its connected.
        - A molecule isn't just a bag of atoms, its atoms bounded in a specific structures. 
        - A social network isn't just a list of users
        - A citt's traffic isn't just a road segments.
The meaning lives in the connection, not just the entities themselves. Classical ML ignores this entirely.

2. What Classical ML assumes(and why it breaks)?
    Every standard algorithm such as SVM, MLP, CNN, RNN assumes our data fits a regular fixed structure.
        - MLP: Fixed length vector inputs
        - CNN: Requires a regular grid
        - RNN: Requires a sequence with a natural order

3. The three core problems GNNs solves
    a. Variable, irregular neighbourhoods: Every node has different number of neighbours in no fixed order.
       GNNs handle this via message passing - each node aggregates information from however many neighbourhood
       it has, using a shared fucntion. No fixed size required.
    b. Structural information must be learned, not discarded:
       Two molecules with identical atom counts but different bonding structures have compleletely different
       properties. A node's identity in a graph depends on where it sits structurally - its neighbours,
       the topology. GNNs encode exactly this by propagating the information across the graph layer by layer.
    c. Generalizastion across graphs:

4. A concrete example
    Take drug discovery. We want to predict whether a molecule is toxic.
        * The molecule has atoms(Carbon, Nitrogen, Oxygen...) connected by bonds(single, double, aromatic...).
        * A CNN sees no grid, An MLP sees no structure. An RNN sees no sequence.
        * A GNN sees exactly what a chemist sees: atoms as nodes with chemical features, bonds as edges with
          bond type features. it passes messges along bonds, aggregating chemical context, and learns which
          structural patterns correlate with toxicity.

    This is why GNNs are used at every major pharmaceutical company today.


5. The core mechanism:
For each node v:
    * gather message from neighbors
    * aggregate them(sum, mean, max ...)
    * update node's own representation
    * repeat for k layers -> captures k-hop neighborhood

7. Three properties of graphs that break CNNs, RNNs, Transformers
    * Variable neighbourhood size: More precise: it's not just graph size but the local fan-in that varies node-by-node
    * Non canonical ordering of neighbours: A pixel has a distinguished north, east, etc. neighbor; the three neighbors of
    a carbon atom do not. Any architecture that indexes neighbors must pick an arbitrary order or pay a factorial cost.
    * Non-Euclidean geometry: Grids and sequences live in Rd with Euclidean distance. Graphs, in general, are not embedded
      in any Euclidean space—the intrinsic metric is shortest-path distance.

8. What are the two symmetries any GNN architecture must respect? Define each precisely.
    1. Permutation Invariance (at the aggregation step)
        Pooling a node's neighbours into a single vector must depend only on the multiset of neighbour features,
        not on their ordering. If you list the neighbours in a different order, you must get the same result.
    2. Permutation Equivariance (at the layer level)
        Relabelling the vertices of the graph must produce a corresponding relabelling of the output features —
        their values stay the same, only their assignment to nodes changes.

    A GNN enforces both symmetries by construction, via message passing.

10. What is a graph? Give the formal definition.
    A graph G = (V, E) is an ordered pair where V is a finite set of vertices (nodes) and E is a set of unordered pairs of distinct
    elements from V called edges. Note: E ⊆ V⁽²⁾ (the set of all 2-element subsets of V). For a directed graph (digraph), E is a set of
    ordered pairs, so (u,v) ≠ (v,u).

12. How is a road network modelled as a graph? What is the role of edge weights?
    V = set of road intersections. 
    E: two intersections are adjacent whenever they are joined directly by a road.
    Edges carry weights representing the distance or time-delay for travelling that road segment.
    Google Maps runs shortest-path algorithms on this graph to compute optimal routes.

13. Why are citation network edges directed? Give the formal definition.
    Citation is a one-way relationship: paper x cites paper y, but y does not necessarily cite x.
    Formally: (x, y) ∈ E if paper x cites paper y. The edge goes from the citing paper to the cited paper.
    The unordered set {x,y} is not used here because direction matters.

14. Describe the Web Graph. What is Google's PageRank and how is it computed on this graph?
    V = set of all URLs of webpages. E: (x, y) is an arc whenever webpage x has a hyperlink to webpage y.
    These are hyperlinks (HTML anchor tags), not physical links.

    PhageRank ranks webpages by the stationary distribution of a random walk on the web graph. Equivalently, it is the principal
    eigenvector (corresponding to eigenvalue λ = 1) of the Google matrix — a column-stochastic version of the adjacency matrix with arc
    teleportation term to handle dangling nodes.

15. What makes a financial transaction graph special compared to a standard graph?
    Three distinguishing properties:

    i. Directed — money flows one way (x transfers to y ≠ y transfers to x)
    ii. Multigraph — multiple transactions can exist between the same pair of accounts
    iii. Edge features — each edge carries amount and timestamp

    Example from slides: In a money-laundering scheme, Sean (criminal) funnels dirty money through a front
    hotel (which also receives legitimate payments from Alice and Bob); the hotel pays shell companies,
    which forward to Tim. A GNN on this graph can detect the suspicious multi-hop pattern.

16. "A large enough MLP on a flattened adjacency matrix can approximate any graph function."
     Is this a valid reason NOT to use GNNs? Explain.

    This is the classic objection, and the answer is: true in principle, misleading in practice.

    * An MLP would have to learn permutation invariance entirely from training data.
    * That requires training examples covering every permutation of every input graph — combinatorially infeasible.
    * A GNN generalises from exponentially less data because the symmetry is built into the architecture.

18. Explain GraphCast. What graph is used? What is the headline result?

    i. Problem: Predict the global atmospheric state up to 10 days ahead. Previously done by ECMWF's HRES                            (numerically solving PDEs on a supercomputer) for 50+ years.
    ii. Graph: Multi-scale icosahedral mesh on the globe. Vertices = points on the sphere.

19. How does Google Maps use GNNs for ETA prediction?

    i. Problem: Predict travel time on road-navigation queries at planet scale.
    ii. Graph: Each road segment is a node (not intersections!). Edges connect segments that meet at an intersection.               Node features: live traffic, time-of-day, speed limit, road category.
    iii. GNN role: Global road graph is partitioned into overlapping Supersegments.
                   A message-passing GNN predicts travel times at multiple horizons per Supersegment.
                   These predictions are composed along the user's route.
    iv. Result: Reduced negative-ETA outcomes by 40%+ in many cities (Sydney 43%, Osaka 37%, Orlando 34%,
                Singapore 31%, Taichung 51%). Serves >1 billion users/month.

20. How was Halicin discovered using a GNN?

    i. Problem: Identify new antibacterial compounds. No new antibiotic class has been discovered since the 1980s.
    ii. Graph: Each molecule = a graph. Atoms = vertices (features: element, charge, hybridization).
               Chemical bonds = edges (features: bond order, stereo).
    iii. GNN role: A D-MPNN (Directed Message Passing Neural Network) trained on 2,335 molecules screened against
                   E. coli, then used to rank a ~6,000-compound repurposing library.
    iv. Result: Identified halicin (SU-3327), active against C. difficile, pan-resistant
                A. baumannii, and M. tuberculosis. Still preclinical — not yet in human trials.

21. Define: degree, in-degree, out-degree. State the Handshaking Lemma and prove it.

    Degree: dᵤ = |N(u)| = Σᵥ A[u,v] (for undirected)
    In-degree: d^in_u = Σᵥ A[v,u] (edges into u)
    Out-degree: d^out_u = Σᵥ A[u,v] (edges out of u)

    Handshaking Lemma: In any undirected graph, Σᵥ∈V dᵥ = 2|E|.
    Proof: Each edge {u,v} contributes exactly 1 to dᵤ and exactly 1 to dᵥ.
    So summing degrees counts each edge exactly twice. ∴ Σ dᵥ = 2|E|. □

23. Define the K-hop neighbourhood N_K(v). How does it connect to GNN depth?
    N_K(v) = {u ∈ V : d_G(u,v) ≤ K}

    where
        d_G(u,v) is the length of the shortest path from u to v (= ∞ if no path exists).

    GNN connection (from slides):
    A K-layer GNN computes the represnttion of node v by aggregating information from v's K-hop neighbourhood N_K(v).
    The depth of the GNN controls how far each node "sees" into the graph.

24. What is graph isomorphism? Why does it matter for GNNs?
    G₁ ≅ G₂ if there exists a bijection φ: V₁ → V₂ such that {u,v} ∈ E₁ ⟺ {φ(u), φ(v)} ∈ E₂.

    Why it matters for GNNs: A maximally powerful GNN should:
        Map isomorphic graphs → same representation
        Map non-isomorphic graphs → different representations

    The Weisfeiler–Lehman (WL) test provides an upper bound on the discriminative power of message-passing GNNs.

25. Distinguish between a heterogeneous graph and a multiplex graph.
    Multi-relational graph: Edges carry types (relations). An edge is a triple (u, τ, v) where τ ∈ ℛ.
                            Each relation type has its own adjacency matrix A_τ.
        i. Heterogeneous graph: Nodes also have types: V = V₁ ∪ V₂ ∪ ... ∪ Vₖ (disjoint).
                             Certain edge types connect only specific node types.
                             Example: Biomedical graph with node types {protein, drug, disease} and
                                                            edge types {treats, interacts, causes}.
        ii. Multiplex graph: Every node belongs to every layer. Each layer = one edge type (intra-layer edges).
                         Plus inter-layer edges connecting the same node across layers.
                         Example: Transportation network where layers = air, rail, road.

26. What is the difference between a simple graph and a multigraph?
    A simple graph has no self-loops and no parallel edges (at most one edge between any pair).
    A multigraph may have multiple (parallel) edges connecting the same two vertices.
    The multiplicity m of {u,v} is the number of edges between them. (Financial transaction graphs are multigraphs.)

27. When is a graph bipartite? Give an equivalent characterisation.
    G = (V,E) is bipartite if V can be partitioned into V₁ and V₂ such that every edge connects a vertex in V₁
    to one in V₂.
    Equivalently: Vertices can be 2-coloured (red/blue) so no two adjacent vertices share the same colour.
                  A graph is not bipartite iff it contains an odd cycle.

33. Eigenvector centrality
    The centrality vector is the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue (the principal eigenvalue).

    Degree centrality treats all neighbors equally, even unimportant ones. Eigenvector centrality captures the
    recursive idea that a node is important if its neighbors are important. Formally, the centrality vector x
    satisfies Ax = κx, where x is the principal eigenvector of the adjacency matrix A.

    Graph: 1 --- 2 --- 3

    Vertices:
    V = {1, 2, 3}

    Edges:
    E = {{1,2}, {2,3}}

    Step 1: Adjacency Matrix

                | 0  1  0 |
            A = | 1  0  1 |
                | 0  1  0 |

    --------------------------------------------------
    
    Step 2: Eigenvector Centrality Equation

        A x = λ x

        where:
            x = (x1, x2, x3)^T

        We use the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue.

    --------------------------------------------------

    Step 3: Find Eigenvalues

        Solve:
            det(A - λI) = 0
            Characteristic polynomial: det(A - λI) = -λ(λ² - 2)

            Set equal to zero: -λ(λ² - 2) = 0

            Therefore:
                λ = 0
                λ = +√2
                λ = -√2

            Largest (principal) eigenvalue: λ1 = √2

    --------------------------------------------------

    Step 4: Solve Ax = √2 x

        Let:
            x = (x1, x2, x3)^T

            Then:
                        | x2      |
                A x =   | x1 + x3 |
                        | x2      |

                Set equal to √2 x:

                | x2      |   | √2 x1 |
                | x1+x3   | = | √2 x2 |
                | x2      |   | √2 x3 |

                This gives:
                    (1) x2 = √2 x1
                    (2) x1 + x3 = √2 x2
                    (3) x2 = √2 x3

    --------------------------------------------------

    Step 5: Find Relationships

        From (1) and (3):  √2 x1 = √2 x3

        Therefore:  x1 = x3

        Let: x1 = x3 = a

        Using (1): x2 = √2 a

        Hence:  x = a(1, √2, 1)^T

    --------------------------------------------------

    Step 6: Normalize the Eigenvector

        Length of (1, √2, 1):

        ||x|| = √(1² + (√2)² + 1²)

             = √(1 + 2 + 1)

             = √4

             = 2

        Divide by 2:

        x = (1/2)(1, √2, 1)^T

    --------------------------------------------------

    Final Eigenvector Centrality Scores

        x = (0.500, 0.707, 0.500)^T

        Node 1 : 0.500
        Node 2 : 0.707
        Node 3 : 0.500

    --------------------------------------------------

    Interpretation:

    - Node 2 has the highest centrality because it is connected to both nodes.
    - Nodes 1 and 3 are symmetric, so they receive equal centrality scores.
    - Therefore:

    Centrality(2) > Centrality(1) = Centrality(3)

34. Why does the Perron–Frobenius theorem guarantee a unique, meaningful eigenvector centrality for connected graphs?
    For a connected graph, A is irreducible. Perron–Frobenius guarantees that the eigenvalue of largest modulus is
    positive (the spectral radius ρ(A)), and its corresponding eigenvector is the only eigenvector with all
    non-negative entries. This makes it canonical — no ambiguity about which eigenvector to pick — and preserves
    recursive semantics.

35. Why does eigenvector centrality fail on DAGs? Give an example.
    On a DAG, source nodes (no in-neighbors) get score 0. By induction, this propagates — only sinks survive with
    nonzero scores. On DAGs with multiple sinks, the null space becomes multi-dimensional and Perron–Frobenius
    uniqueness breaks down.

    Example:
    Citation networks are DAGs (x cites y ⟹ x published after y). Most nodes get score 0, making the measure useless.

36. Define Katz centrality and explain what it counts.
    On a directed graph, eigenvector centrality says that node i is important if its in-neighbors are important:

    xi = Σ xj
        j:j→i

    Equivalently, xi = Σ Aji xj

    or in matrix form: Aᵀ x = λx

    The principal eigenvector of Aᵀ gives the centrality scores.
    
    x = (I↗ ϱ A↑)↗1 1.

    Issue:
        If a node u has no in-neighbors, then xu = 0. Similarly, if u → v is v's only incoming edge, then xv = 0.
        By induction, on a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), this recursion drives most scores to zero. Only sinks
        survive, and they correspond to eigenvalue 0 rather than the positive principal eigenvalue that the
        Perron–Frobenius theorem would otherwise guarantee.

    As a result, the eigenvector-centrality framework becomes degenerate on DAGs.

37. Katz Centrality

    Definition.
        Let G be a directed graph with adjacency matrix A. The Katz centrality scores of the nodes are

        x = (I − αAᵀ)^(-1) 1

    What does this mean?

        Use the Neumann series: (I − αAᵀ)^(-1) = Σ α^k (Aᵀ)^k
                                                k=0→∞

        (valid for |α| < 1/ρ(A); note that ρ(Aᵀ) = ρ(A))

    Reading off:
        xi = Σ α^k ((Aᵀ)^k 1)i
             k=0→∞

           = Σ α^k · #{walks of length k ending at i}
             k=0→∞

    Under our convention, ((Aᵀ)^k)ij counts length-k walks
    from j to i, so (Aᵀ)^k 1 at position i counts walks
    ending at i.

    Interpretation:
    A node is important if many walks of evry lngth end at it, with shorter walks weighted more heavily (factor α^k).

    Hyperparameter α: α must be chosen below 1/ρ(A) for convergence.


38. Example: Katz Centrality on a 3-Node DAG (1/3)

    Three vertices with edges: 1 → 2, 1 → 3, 2 → 3

    With Aij = 1 iff i → j (CLRS convention):

            [0 1 1]
    A   =   [0 0 1]
            [0 0 0]

            [0 0 0]
    Aᵀ  =   [1 0 0]
            [1 1 0]

    A is nilpotent (A³ = 0), with spectrum {0, 0, 0}.

    Eigenvector centrality is degenerate, not undefined.

    The principal eigenvalue is 0, and solving

    Aᵀx = 0

    gives x = (0, 0, 1)ᵀ

    Only the sink (node 3) receives a nonzero score.

    For DAGs with multiple sinks, the null space becomes multi-dimensional and Perron–Frobenius uniqueness breaks.
    There is no longer a canonical eigenvector to call "the" centrality.

    Katz to the rescue.
    
    Choose α = 1/2 (any α > 0 works, since Aᵀ is nilpotent).

    The Neumann series is

    (I − αAᵀ)^(-1) = Σ α^k (Aᵀ)^k
                     k=0→∞

    Since (Aᵀ)^k = 0 for k ≥ 3, the series collapses to a finite sum.

    With α = 1/2 and (Aᵀ)^k = 0 for k ≥ 3,

    (I − 1/2 Aᵀ)^(-1) = I + 1/2 Aᵀ + 1/4 (Aᵀ)^2

    Compute the squared term:

             [0 0 0]
    (Aᵀ)^2 = [0 0 0]
             [1 0 0]

    Add the three weighted matrices:

          [1 0 0]
    I   = [0 1 0]
          [0 0 1]

                [0   0   0]
    1/2 Aᵀ =    [1/2 0   0]
                [1/2 1/2 0]

                    [0   0 0]
    1/4(Aᵀ)^2 =     [0   0 0]
                    [1/4 0 0]

    Sum:

    [1   0   0]
    [1/2 1   0]
    [3/4 1/2 1]

    Multiply by the all-ones vector:

    x = (I − 1/2 Aᵀ)^(-1) 1 = (1, 3/2, 9/4)ᵀ

    The score vector  x = (1, 3/2, 9/4)ᵀ makes structural sense.

    Walk-counting interpretation: Each xi counts walks ending at node i, weighted by

    α^k = (1/2)^k

        Node 1:
            - No incoming walks.
            - Only the length-0 walk.

            x1 = 1

        Node 2:
            - One length-0 walk.
            - One length-1 walk: 1 → 2

            x2 = 1 + 1/2
               = 3/2

        Node 3:
            - One length-0 walk.
            - Two length-1 walks:
                1 → 3
                2 → 3
            - One length-2 walk:
                1 → 2 → 3

                x3 = 1 + 1 + 1/4
                   = 9/4

    Thus, x = (1, 3/2, 9/4)ᵀ

39. What is the key problem with Katz centrality that PageRank fixes? How does PageRank fix it?
    In Katz centrality, a highly important node (for example, amazon.com) passes its full weight to all of its
    out-neighbors. As a result, pages can receive inflated scores simply because they are linked from a very
    popular hub.

    PageRank fixes this by dividing the score passed to each out-neighbor by the source node's out-degree
    d_j^out. This conserves the total importance (mass) being distributed.

    The PageRank equation is:

                    x_j
    x_i = α Σ ---------------- + (1 − α)
           j:j→i   d_j^out

        where:
            - α is the damping factor (typically α ≈ 0.85),
            - d_j^out is the out-degree of node j.

    Interpretation:
        Instead of giving its entire score to every outgoing neighbor, a node splits its score equally among all
    outgoing links. This prevents high-degree hubs from artificially boosting the scores of all pages they point to.

    Equivalently, PageRank can be viewed as the stationary distribution of a random walk with teleportation.
    At each step:
        1. With probability α, follow an outgoing link.
        2. With probability (1 − α), teleport to a random page.

    This normalization and teleportation make PageRank more robust and meaningful than Katz centrality on large web graphs.

40. Why is teleportation necessary in PageRank?
    Without teleportation, the random walk gets stuck in dangling nodes or sink components. Teleportation makes the Markov
    chain irreducible and aperiodic, guaranteeing a unique stationary distribution exists.

43. Define the global clustering coefficient. What does C = 0 and C = 1 mean?
    The global clustering coefficient is defined as:

    C = (# closed 2-paths) / (# 2-paths)
      = (3 × # triangles) / (# connected triples)

        where:
            - A closed 2-path is a 2-path whose endpoints are connected, forming a triangle.
            - A connected triple is a set of three nodes connected by at least two edges.

    Properties:
        - C ∈ [0, 1]

    Interpretation:
        - C = 0 : No triangles exist in the network.
        - C = 1 : Every connected component is a clique (every possible triangle exists).

    Empirical observation:
        - Real social networks typically have C ≈ 0.2.
        - Random graphs of the same size typically have C ≈ 10⁻⁴.

44. Example: Global Clustering Coefficient on a Triangle-with-Tail

          (1)
           | \
           |  (3) ---- (4) ---- (5)
           | /
          (2)

        Nodes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5
        Edges: {1, 2}, {1, 3}, {2, 3}, {3, 4}, {4, 5}
        Degrees: d1 = 2, d2 = 2, d3 = 3, d4 = 2, d5 = 1

        Triangles (T):
        -----------------
        A triangle consists of 3 mutually adjacent nodes.
        Only {1, 2, 3} forms a triangle.
        Total Triangles (T) = 1

        Total Ordered 2-Paths:
        -------------------------
        Formula for ordered 2-paths centered at node v: dv * (dv - 1)

        Sum over all nodes v:
        = d1(d1-1) + d2(d2-1) + d3(d3-1) + d4(d4-1) + d5(d5-1)
        = 2(1) + 2(1) + 3(2) + 2(1) + 1(0)
        = 2 + 2 + 6 + 2 + 0
        = 12

        Closed (Ordered) 2-Paths:
        ----------------------------
        Each triangle {a, b, c} yields 6 closed 2-paths: 
        abc, acb, bac, bca, cab, cba

        Total closed 2-paths = 6 * T
                             = 6 * 1 
                             = 6

        Global Clustering Coefficient (C):
        -------------------------------------
        C = (Closed Ordered 2-Paths) / (Total Ordered 2-Paths)
        C = 6 / 12
        C = 1 / 2


        Sanity Check via Connected Triples:
        --------------------------------------
        Number of connected triples (K_1,2 subgraphs) = 6
        Alternative formula: C = (3 * T) / (Number of connected triples)
        C = (3 * 1) / 6 = 3 / 6 = 1 / 2

46. What are the 4 rooted graphlets on ≤3 vertices? Why does P₃ have two rooted graphlets but K₂ and K₃ each have only one?
    The four rooted graphlets are:

    γ₀: K₂ (edge) — Number of edges touching the node (this is just the standard node degree).
    γ₁: P₃ endpoint — Number of induced 3-node paths (P₃) where the node is an end-point (e.g., u - v - w).
    γ₂: P₃ middle — Number of induced 3-node paths (P₃) where the node is the center/hub (e.g., v - u - w).
    γ₃: K₃ (triangle) — Number of triangles (C₃) the node is part of.

49. 1-Weisfeiler-Lehman (1-WL) Graph Isomorphism Test
    The 1-WL test is an algorithm used to determine if two graphs are structurally identical (isomorphic). It works
    by iteratively updating a node's label based on its current label and the labels of its neighbors, then compressing
    this information into a unique hash value. If at any point the collection (multiset) of labels in graph G does not
    match the collection of labels in graph G', the algorithm stops and correctly states that the graphs are not isomorphic.

52. Example: Five Local Overlap Measures on the Bowtie Graph

        Graph Visualization:
        --------------------
          (1)         (5)
           | \       / |
           |  (3) --   
           | /       \ |
          (2)         (4)


        Target Evaluation:
        ------------------
        Pair to score: Non-adjacent pair {1, 4}

        Base Neighborhood Data:
        - N(1) = {2, 3}  --> Degree d1 = 2
        - N(4) = {3, 5}  --> Degree d4 = 2

        Overlaps:
        - Intersection: N(1) ^ N(4) = {3}    --> Size = 1
        - Union:        N(1) v N(4) = {2,3,5}--> Size = 3


        Calculations:
        -------------
        1. Common Neighbors (Scn): Simply counts the number of shared neighbors.
           Formula: |N(1) ^ N(4)|
           Scn[1, 4] = 1

        2. Jaccard Coefficient (SJaccard): Measures the ratio of shared neighbors to the total unique neighbors combined.
           Formula: |N(1) ^ N(4)| / |N(1) v N(4)|
           SJaccard[1, 4] = 1 / 3

        3. Sorensen-Dice Coefficient (SSorensen): Compares the shared neighbors against the sum of the individual degrees.
           Formula: (2 * |N(1) ^ N(4)|) / (d1 + d4)
           SSorensen[1, 4] = (2 * 1) / (2 + 2) = 2 / 4 = 1 / 2

        4. Salton Index / Cosine Similarity (SSalton): Normalizes the common neighbors by the geometric mean of the node degrees.
           Formula: |N(1) ^ N(4)| / sqrt(d1 * d4)
           SSalton[1, 4] = 1 / sqrt(2 * 2) = 1 / 2

        5. Resource Allocation Index (SRA):
           Formula: Sum over shared neighbors of (1 / degree(shared_node))
           Shared node is 3, and d3 = 4.
           SRA[1, 4] = 1 / d3 = 1 / 4

        6. Adamic-Adar Index (SAA):
           Formula: Sum over shared neighbors of (1 / log(degree(shared_node)))
           SAA = 1 / log(4) approx 0.721

53. Why does deeper WL generally give lower similarity between non-isomorphic graphs?
    At depth 0, the kernel only compares degree distributions. At depth K it compares multisets of K-hop rooted subtrees —
    a strictly richer description. Two graphs that appear identical at low depths can become distinguishable at higher depths
    (provided 1-WL can distinguish them at all). Going from depth 1 to depth 2 for P₅ vs P₃ drops the cosine from ≈0.74 to ≈0.51.

54. What is the forward connection between the WL kernel and GNNs?
    The WL iteration — aggregate neighbor labels, hash to a new label — is structurally identical to a message-passing GNN layer.
    Replacing the injective hash with a differentiable neural network gives the GIN architecture (Xu et al. 2019), which is exactly 
    as expressive as 1-WL. The WL kernel is therefore the non-parametric ancestor of the MPNN.

55. What is the "celebrity problem" in common neighbors, and how do RA and AA fix it?
    Two pairs both sharing 2 mutual friends get the same SCN score, even if one shared friend is a celebrity with 10⁶ connections
    (where overlap is nearly random) and the other is an obscure person (where overlap is highly informative).
    RA weights each common neighbor w by 1/dw — linear suppression. A hub with dw=1000 contributes only 0.001.
    AA weights by 1/log(dw) — gentler, logarithmic suppression. The same hub contributes ≈0.145.
    Both give more credit to rare, low-degree common neighbors.

56. What is the fundamental limitation of all local overlap measures?
    They can only see common neighbors (1-hop overlap). Two pairs with SCN=0 (no common neighbors) may be at very different graph
    distances — one reachable through a length-3 path, another not at all — but local measures cannot distinguish them.
    This motivates global overlap measures (Katz index, Leicht–Holme–Newman, personalized PageRank) that aggregate over paths
    of every length using (I-κM)⁻¹ expressions.

57. Why does the GDV of node 3 in the bowtie have γ₁=0?
    γ₁ counts induced P₃ subgraphs where node 3 is an endpoint. For 3 to be a P₃ endpoint, it needs a neighbor u, and u must
    have a neighbor outside {3}∪N(3). But N(3)={1,2,4,5} = V{3}, so no vertex exists outside 3's closed neighborhood. Hence γ₁(3)=0.

58. State the web-spam detection result of Becchetti et al. and three lessons learned.
    Using 163 link-based features (degree, PageRank, neighborhood counts) fed into a C4.5 decision tree with bagging, 
    they achieved F₁=0.871 on UK-2002 and 0.631 on UK-2006 — comparable to the best content-based classifiers.

    Three lessons:
    * Hand-crafted node features carry serious signal at scale
    * Different feature families (degree, centrality, distance-graded counts) are non-redundant — they combine multiplicatively
    * These features encode structural hypotheses that GNNs later learn (Truncated PageRank is a hand-coded message-passing scheme)

60. SECTION 3: THE UNNORMALIZED LAPLACIAN -- PROPERTIES

    DEFINITION:
      L := D - A

      Where:
        D = diag(d_1, d_2, ..., d_n)  (degree matrix)
        A = adjacency matrix

    CONCRETE ENTRIES:
      L[u,u] = d_u          (diagonal = degree)
      L[u,w] = -1           if {u,w} in E
      L[u,w] =  0           if {u,w} not in E (and u =/= w)

    EXAMPLE: BOWTIE GRAPH (vertices 1,2,3,4,5 with vertex 3 as hub)
      Degrees: d_1=2, d_2=2, d_3=4, d_4=2, d_5=2

      L = [ 2  -1  -1   0   0 ]
          [-1   2  -1   0   0 ]
          [-1  -1   4  -1  -1 ]
          [ 0   0  -1   2  -1 ]
          [ 0   0  -1  -1   2 ]

      Every row sums to 0. L is symmetric.

    FIVE KEY PROPERTIES (KNOW ALL FIVE):

      (i)  SYMMETRY: L^T = L
           Proof: D and A are each symmetric, so D-A is symmetric.

      (ii) DIRICHLET ENERGY IDENTITY (THE MOST IMPORTANT FORMULA):
           For every x in R^n:

           x^T L x = (1/2) * sum_{(u,v) in VxV} A[u,v]*(x_u - x_v)^2
                   = sum_{(u,v) in E} (x_u - x_v)^2

           Interpretation: x^T L x is the TOTAL SQUARED EDGE VARIATION of
           the signal x: V -> R. This is called the DIRICHLET ENERGY.

      (iii) POSITIVE SEMI-DEFINITE (PSD):
            x^T L x >= 0 for all x in R^n
            Proof: Each term (x_u - x_v)^2 in (ii) is non-negative.

      (iv) EIGENVALUES are real and non-negative:
           Order them as: 0 = lambda_1 <= lambda_2 <= ... <= lambda_n
           (Proof: L is real symmetric by (i), PSD by (iii))

      (v)  L*1 = 0  (the all-ones vector 1 is always an eigenvector with eigenvalue 0)
           Proof: For each vertex u:
             (L*1)_u = sum_v L[u,v]*1 = d_u + sum_{v~u}(-1) = d_u - d_u = 0

61. SECTION 11: IMPORTANT FORMULAS CHEAT SHEET

    L = D - A                         (definition, unnormalized)
    L = B^T B                         (factorization)
    x^T L x = sum_{(u,v) in E} (x_u - x_v)^2   (Dirichlet energy)
    L * 1 = 0                         (all-ones vector is in ker(L))
    dim ker(L) = K                    (number of connected components)
    lambda_2 > 0  <=>  G connected    (Fiedler criterion)

    L_sym = D^{-1/2} L D^{-1/2}      (symmetric normalized)
    L_rw  = D^{-1} L                 (random-walk normalized)

    RatioCut(A_1,...,A_K) = sum_k [cut_k / |A_k|]
    NCut(A_1,...,A_K)     = sum_k [cut_k / vol(A_k)]

    Spectral relaxation lower bound: |V| * lambda_2 <= |V| * min RatioCut

    SETUP (Two Triangles Joined by Two Bridges):
      V = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}

        Triangle 1: vertices {1,2,3} (edges {1,2},{1,3},{2,3})

        Bridge edges: {1,4} and {3,6}

      Degrees: (d_1, d_2, d_3, d_4, d_5, d_6) = (3, 2, 3, 3, 2, 3)

      Partition              | |A| | cut | RatioCut
      -----------------------+-----+-----+---------------------------
      {1,2,3} | {4,5,6}      |  3  |  2  | 2/3 + 2/3 = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

      The singleton {2} has the SAME raw cut (2) as the natural partition!
      But its RatioCut (2.40) is much worse than the natural split (1.33).

63. SECTION 9: WORKED EXAMPLE -- THE SPECTRAL METHOD (FULL SOLUTION)

    Using the same two-triangles-two-bridges graph.


                   (1)=================(4)
                  / |                   | \
                 /  |                   |  \
               (2)  |                   |  (5)
                 \  |                   |  /
                  \ |                   | /
                   (3)=================(6)

    --- STEP 1: BUILD L ---
        Degrees: (3, 2, 3, 3, 2, 3)

        L = [  3  -1  -1  -1   0   0 ]
            [ -1   2  -1   0   0   0 ]
            [ -1  -1   3   0   0  -1 ]
            [ -1   0   0   3  -1  -1 ]
            [  0   0   0  -1   2  -1 ]
            [  0   0  -1  -1  -1   3 ]

        Checks:
          - Symmetric? YES
          - Every row sums to 0? YES
          - Diagonal entries = degrees? YES
          - Off-diagonal = -A[u,v]? YES

    --- STEP 2: EIGENDECOMPOSITION ---
        Spec(L) = {0, 1, 3, 3, 4, 5}

          lambda_1 = 0   (multiplicity 1 --> G is CONNECTED)
          lambda_2 = 1   (FIEDLER VALUE = algebraic connectivity)
          lambda_3 = 3
          lambda_4 = 3
          lambda_5 = 4
          lambda_6 = 5

        Spectral gap: lambda_2 - lambda_1 = 1 - 0 = 1

    --- STEP 3: FIEDLER VECTOR ---
        Eigenvector for lambda_2 = 1 (rescaled so largest magnitude is +/-2, first entry positive):

          a* = (1, 2, 1, -1, -2, -1)^T

        VERIFICATION (check that L * a* = 1 * a* = a*):
          Row 1: 3(1) + (-1)(2) + (-1)(1) + (-1)(-1) + 0(-2) + 0(-1)
                 = 3 - 2 - 1 + 1 = 1 = a*_1  CORRECT

          Row 2: (-1)(1) + 2(2) + (-1)(1) + 0(-1) + 0(-2) + 0(-1)
                 = -1 + 4 - 1 = 2 = a*_2  CORRECT

          (You can verify remaining rows similarly)

        STRUCTURE ANALYSIS:
          - Interior vertices 2 and 5 (connected only to same-triangle neighbors)
            --> sit at EXTREMES: a*_2 = +2, a*_5 = -2
          - Boundary vertices 1,3,4,6 (each has a bridge edge to opposite cluster)
            --> sit CLOSER TO ZERO: a*_1=+1, a*_3=+1, a*_4=-1, a*_6=-1
          - The Fiedler vector "SOFTENS" near the cut, most polarized at cluster cores

    --- STEP 4: SIGN-BASED ROUNDING ---
        Signs of a* = (1, 2, 1, -1, -2, -1)^T:
          Vertex 1: +1  --> A
          Vertex 2: +2  --> A
          Vertex 3: +1  --> A
          Vertex 4: -1  --> A_bar
          Vertex 5: -2  --> A_bar
          Vertex 6: -1  --> A_bar

        SPECTRAL BIPARTITION:
          A     = {1, 2, 3}
          A_bar = {4, 5, 6}

        cut(A, A_bar) = 2  (the two bridge edges {1,4} and {3,6})
        RatioCut      = 2/3 + 2/3 = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

        This MATCHES the brute-force optimum! The algorithm recovered the correct partition.

    --- STEP 5: RELAXATION GAP ---
        Spectral lower bound:  |V| * lambda_2 = 6 * 1 = 6
        True discrete optimum: |V| * min RatioCut = 6 * (4/3) = 8

        Relative gap = (8-6)/8 = 25%

        The relaxation is NOT TIGHT (25% gap), but the ROUNDED solution still finds
        the true optimum. This is the TYPICAL SITUATION for spectral methods.



MISTAKE 1: Confusing eigenvalue SMALLEST with PageRank (which uses LARGEST).
  Remember: Laplacian uses SMALLEST non-trivial eigenvalue lambda_2.
  PageRank uses LARGEST eigenvalue of the transition matrix.

MISTAKE 2: Forgetting that the orientation of B doesn't matter.
  B^T B = L regardless of how you orient the edges.

MISTAKE 3: Confusing ker(L) = K components with saying "L has K eigenvalues = 0".
  Both statements are equivalent -- the MULTIPLICITY of eigenvalue 0 is K.

MISTAKE 4: In the RatioCut formula, forgetting the denominator applies PER CLUSTER.
  RatioCut = sum over ALL k of [cut_k / |A_k|], not [total cut / |V|].

MISTAKE 5: Thinking the spectral bipartition algorithm finds the EXACT minimum.
  It finds an APPROXIMATE minimum. It is a heuristic with no multiplicative guarantee.
  The lower bound is |V|*lambda_2, not the true optimum.

MISTAKE 6: Forgetting WHY we need a perp 1 in the relaxation.
  Without it, the minimizer is the trivial constant (all vertices in same cluster).
  The orthogonality constraint FORCES us to a non-trivial eigenvector.

MISTAKE 7: Confusing L_sym and L_rw.
  L_sym = D^{-1/2} L D^{-1/2}  (SYMMETRIC)
  L_rw  = D^{-1} L              (NOT symmetric in general)
  They are similar matrices and share the same eigenvalues.
